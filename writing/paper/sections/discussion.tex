The analysis distinguishes what the data can identify directly from what requires counterfactual modeling. Descriptive data mining reveals rainfall timing, speed-deficit abnormality, endogenous recovery, spatial concentration, and OD functional dependence. It cannot by itself identify the additional loss that would have been reduced by unobserved interventions. The optimization model supplies this counterfactual probe, and the learning analysis then compresses the optimizer-derived value field into interpretable laws.

The resulting laws are conditional rather than universal constants. They are learned under a standardized recovery regime: fixed intervention primitives, effectiveness assumptions, costs, deployment caps, response delays, budget intensity, and piecewise-linear diminishing returns. Within that regime, the findings are stable: first-order marginal value follows an activated exposure--horizon--efficiency form, while complete finite-budget allocation requires residual re-scoring to avoid saturation and overlapping effects. The non-obvious action-law analysis further shows that high deficit, high exposure, and high static bottleneck scores are not sufficient recovery rules; static structure acts as latent leverage only when it overlaps with persistent future loss, relevant OD exposure, feasible timing, and efficient intervention. The representative scenario-optimum validation further shows that this residual law remains close to non-base LP optima when budget and delay change. The budget phase analysis adds a useful negative result: the present low/base/high scan does not show an interior peak in absolute decision leverage, but it does show diminishing leverage per unit budget.

The surrogate evidence also argues for parsimony. In the factorized leave-one-city tests, the largest decision-centered gains come from adding OD exposure and time-feasibility activation, while unrestricted high-dimensional interaction terms do not improve the full model. The event-regime tests point in the same direction: the factorized law remains close to the full additive surrogate when entire rainfall, impact, duration, loss, and time-of-day regimes are held out. The OD graph ablation adds a sharper structural result: preserving city-level graph-feature distributions while shuffling their unit alignment removes much of the top-tail recovery-value signal. Thus city structure matters through actual OD spatial alignment, not only through aggregate exposure concentration. This does not rule out useful graph neural representations, but it changes their role: a graph surrogate should be used to test higher-order road or OD message passing, not because the current action-value law requires an opaque high-capacity model.

The main empirical message is therefore not that the largest disruptions are always the most recoverable. In the current sample, event-level decision-criticality is more closely tied to marginal-value top-tail concentration than to no-intervention loss magnitude. This supports a decision-centered definition of recoverable resilience: events are most valuable for management when addressable loss is concentrated on a small set of exposed, intervention-sensitive locations.

The early-window analysis clarifies the operational boundary. Decision-criticality has early signals under leave-city validation, especially through static city structure and abnormal speed in the first hours, but recoverable fraction remains more dependent on the full observed trajectory. Therefore the present study should not claim full online dispatch capability. It establishes hindsight counterfactual recoverability and shows that some of its event-level signals may be partially visible early.

Several boundaries remain. The current sample contains seven full-zone cities with usable speed-event overlap. Scenario-specific LP closure is representative rather than exhaustive because several hard non-base cases reached the solver time limit. Intervention effectiveness and cost parameters are not causally identified from observed intervention records. The event-regime validation is stronger than a random split, but a clean leave-time-period-out test is not yet available because years are strongly confounded with cities in the current sample. Finally, the present graph result is an OD-dependency alignment ablation, not a full road-adjacency or graph-neural closure. A richer graph surrogate would strengthen the spatial representation test in a later version, but the current symbolic law is already reproducible from action-token evidence.
