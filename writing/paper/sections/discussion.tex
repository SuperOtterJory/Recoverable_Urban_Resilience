The discussion will distinguish what the data can identify directly from what requires counterfactual modeling. Descriptive data mining can reveal disruption, heterogeneity, endogenous recovery, and functional dependence. It cannot by itself identify the additional recovery achievable under unobserved interventions.

The first optimization results suggest that the counterfactual model contains learnable structure suitable for later law extraction. In the small scenario-sweep experiments, budget intensity and response delay form a stable response surface for recoverable functional loss. However, these relationships are partly induced by the scenario design itself and should not be treated as the main urban law. The large-scale full-zone analysis therefore fixes the recovery regime and compares cities under the same budget intensity, delays, intervention efficiencies, deployment caps, and diminishing-return assumptions. Under this fixed regime, optimized allocation still outperforms damage-based, exposure-based, and access-based heuristic policies, which provides an empirical measure of decision leverage.

The most relevant preliminary law is therefore structural rather than purely parametric: recoverability and decision leverage appear to vary with the scale and sparsity of functional dependence, rainfall-event impact, speed-deficit severity, congestion exposure, and the concentration of observed deficit burden. The current optimized sample contains seven full-zone cities, so these patterns should be interpreted as hypotheses for later law extraction rather than as final universal laws. The next empirical step is to expand the city sample and test whether these structural relationships remain stable after controlling for calibration choices and intervention-regime assumptions.
