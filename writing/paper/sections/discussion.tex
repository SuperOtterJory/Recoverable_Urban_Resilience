The discussion will distinguish what the data can identify directly from what requires counterfactual modeling. Descriptive data mining can reveal disruption, heterogeneity, endogenous recovery, and functional dependence. It cannot by itself identify the additional recovery achievable under unobserved interventions.

The first optimization results suggest that the counterfactual model contains learnable structure suitable for later law extraction. Budget intensity and response delay form a stable response surface for recoverable functional loss; optimized allocation also outperforms damage-based, exposure-based, and access-based heuristic policies, which provides an empirical measure of decision leverage. These patterns support the paper's methodological sequence: use optimization to generate counterfactual recovery decisions, use learning models to compress the decision structure, and then use interpretability or symbolic regression to search for lower-dimensional recovery laws.

At the same time, the current evidence should be interpreted carefully. The strongest law-like signal is scenario-conditioned rather than universal across cities. Cross-city structural variables such as speed-deficit severity, demand concentration, and deficit-burden concentration are plausible explanatory variables, but the current optimized sample is still too small to claim a transferable urban law from them alone.
